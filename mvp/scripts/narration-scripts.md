## 1. Neem (Code: NM01)

You're standing beside one of India's most remarkable trees — the Neem.

For over four thousand years, villages across India have called this tree their pharmacy. Its leaves treat skin infections. Its twigs clean teeth. Its oil repels insects. Even today, millions of people use Neem daily without ever visiting a doctor.

But here's what's truly extraordinary. A single mature Neem can produce up to thirty kilograms of fruit every year. Birds eat the fruit, carry the seeds, and spread the tree across vast distances. That's why you'll find Neem growing everywhere from Rajasthan's deserts to the coasts of Tamil Nadu.

The next time you see Neem leaves scattered on the ground, remember — each leaf carries compounds that scientists are still studying today. Ancient wisdom confirmed by modern science. One tree. A thousand uses.

## 2. Banyan (Code: BN02)

What you're looking at might seem like a grove of trees. But look closer — it's actually one single organism.

The Banyan is India's national tree, and for good reason. It starts as a tiny seed, often deposited by a bird in the crack of another tree. As it grows, it sends down aerial roots — thin tendrils that reach for the ground. Once they touch soil, they thicken into pillars, becoming new trunks.

One famous Banyan in Kolkata's botanical garden covers over three and a half acres. It looks like a forest, but it's one tree with over three thousand aerial roots.

In Indian culture, the Banyan represents eternal life. Village councils have met under Banyans for centuries — the spreading canopy providing shade for hundreds of people at once. A living community hall, growing wider with each passing decade.

## 3. Gulmohar (Code: GM03)

Every April, this tree does something spectacular. It drops all its leaves — and then explodes into colour.

The Gulmohar — also called the Flame Tree — is not originally from India. It traveled here from Madagascar, an island off the coast of Africa, brought by colonial traders in the eighteen hundreds. It found the Indian heat perfect, and spread across every city in the south.

Look at the flowers closely. Each petal is the size of a coin, with streaks of yellow and orange fading into deep red. When they fall, they carpet the ground like embers from a fire.

But here's the detail most people miss. The Gulmohar's leaves are incredibly fine — almost like fern fronds. Hundreds of tiny leaflets on each branch, creating the most delicate dappled shade you'll find anywhere in Hyderabad.

It blooms for just two months. You're seeing it at exactly the right time.
